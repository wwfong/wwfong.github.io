I owned product for [Topline](https://www.abbeyroad.com/apps), Abbey Road's mobile songwriting app. The thread running through the work was giving songwriters higher controllability over the audio they were capturing on a phone, closer to what they'd expect from a desktop DAW.

The audio features I shepherded into the live app were a mixer panel for per-track level and routing, a convolution reverb with a library of impulse responses to choose from, a mono-to-stereo widener (important: songwriters typically capture mono on phone mics, but the reference they imagine is always stereo), and Bluetooth connectivity for external mics and headphones. Each shipped feature mapped to measurable lifts in engagement and retention.

Most of the interesting work was at the seams: getting decent latency and signal quality out of a mobile audio stack that wasn't built for tracking, and exposing the right surface of controls to creators who don't think in audio-engineering terms.

The launch of these features was [announced on the Abbey Road blog](https://www.abbeyroad.com/news/topline-app-releases-new-features-for-artists-songwriters-2797).
